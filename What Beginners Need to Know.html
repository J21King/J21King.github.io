Before I started taking lessons, it never crossed my mind to learn about the important details of tennis, like how to hold a racquet properly or even if my technique was correct. I just went out and played. Now, I've realized I was wrong, and that what you don't know can hurt you. Tennis elbow is defined as "localized pain over the lateral epicondylitis". One cause is from improper tennis techniques and it can permanently put your tennis future to rest. That is not to say that just by having proper technique, you won't get tennis elbow however. Repetitive stress on the muscles around the lateral epicondylitis and aging also cause tennis elbow. These natural causes cannot be helped, but they won't cause tennis elbow as rapidly as poor technique. If you start to feel the symptoms of tennis elbow (pain in the outer part of the elbow, pain in wrist extension, pain when lifting with palms face down) you should stop playing tennis and anything else that causes stress to your arm. Rest is the only guaranteed way to heal tennis elbow, because tennis elbow is caused by microtearing of the muscles. In more serious cases, ice, a massage, and a splint may help, but if it's too serious there's nothing that can remedy it except surgery. This page will discuss the minor details in tennis that prevent major injuries.

1. Grip Types

It cannot be stressed enough how important it is to have the proper grip when holding a racquet. 80% of the time, beginning tennis players eventually develop tennis elbow/tendinitis due to improperly holding the racquet and failing to correct it. Note that when someone refers to the grip they are using, they are referring to the forehand grip. The forehand versions of grips are the commonly known ones, but there are backhand versions that are a bit different from their forehand counterparts. For simplicity's sake, I will only mention the forehand versions. For one-hand backhands you can use a Continental grip, turning the racquet more counterclockwise to increase topspin. For two-handeD backhands, place your left hand above your right hand; the left are is the dominant arm in a two-handed backhand. Your right hand should be using the Eastern or Semi-Western grips for flatter hits, and a Continental grip for more topspin.

Continental
The Continental grip is best suited for serving, volleying, and slicing. As mentioned before, it is also the choice grip for a one-handed backhanders. It helps lock your wrist and keeps the natural flow of your arm, particularly when serving. Knowing the advantages of this grip, you need to know that this grip cannot generate any topspin or power in groundstrokes, so don't use it in baseline rallies. To hold this grip, have the plane, or face, of the strings perpindicular to the ground. Then place the palm of your hand 45o to the right of the topside of the handle and on the bevel. The top surface should be narrower than the two surfaces to the sides. Being able to use this grip properly will greatly reduce the stress on your wrist. In addition, this grip is a very commonly used by pros, so get familiar with this one.
Eastern
The Easten grip is the "classic" grip. It is often taught to beginning students, because it flows naturally with the movement of the arm and body. The grip is also very versatile, allowing the player to hit flat, topspin, and weak slices with a slight tilt of the racquet face. This grip is more proficient from the forehand side and hitting flat balls than it is on the backhand side and hitting with spin. To use this grip, have the plane of the racquet face parallel to the ground, and place the palm of your hand on the topside of the handle. This surface should be the wider side, the exact opposite of the Cintinental grip. On a side note, the decreased amount of spin from using this grip deters pros from using the Eastern grip, and instead they use a more Western grip.
Semi-Western
The Semi-Western grip is what many pros like to use now, because it can generate more topspin than the Eastern grip, but at the same time, not greatly limit the variety of shots that can be made. This grip does prevent you from slicing, or slicing well, but it does great against high balls. High balls are more common now, because of the improving kick serves and topspin hits. To hold this grip, follow the instructions for an Eastern grip (plane of strings parallel to the ground, palm on wider top surface) then move your hand 45o clockwise so the palm of your hand rests on the bevel. The face of the racquet would naturally tilt downwards a bit, so you must contact the ball earlier and swing upwards more sharply. This is what causes the added topspin.
Western
The Western grip isn't as common as the Semi-Western or Eastern grips, but there are great players that use the Western grip, a prime example being Rafael Nadal. This grip totally prevents the user from slicing in any way possible, and can cause some difficulty when returnting low shots. Like the Semi-Western, high balls are this grip's favourite target. To hold this grip, start with the Semi-Western grip and turn your hand another 45o to the right. The palm of your hand should rest on the narrower, bottom plane of the racquet. It is also a full 90o to the right of the Eastern grip. This racquet face is severly tilted downwards, so the ball must be met very early and with a fast, upward stroke. This causes extreme topspin on the ball.

2. Types of Shots

Beginners usually don't even care about what kind of shots they make, they just want the ball to stay in so they can get a rally going. As beginners get better, they become intermediate players, and like to mix up their shots to keep their opponent from being able to guess the shot and responding to it quicker than expected. Then, when you become an advanced player, hitting a variety of shots should be second nature, as well as having perfect judgement to when and where you should direct a type of shot. I'm starting to aim my shots, but at the same time, I'm still working on getting them over as well. Practice makes perfect =P.

Flat/Drive
Flat hits can be called the plain hits, there's no arm rotation or fancy footwork, it just a swing and a hit. Of course you must still follow the basic style for hitting a tennis ball. 70% of the power in a shot comes from the legs, so make sure you bend those legs. Generally, taking your racquet back far and being able to generate pace is good, but if you really can't, no worries. That's why they manufacture light racquets with more power; many people have short swings. Beginners usually start with short swings and later perfect their technique in order to be able to take larger swings, but if you feel having a short swing is the way to go, no one will argue. Meet the ball in front of your body and not to the side, and accelerate the racquet upon impact with the ball. Try to swing parallel to the ground. A drive is basically a flat with more power and more court depth, so that it lands around the baseline.
Topspin
Without topspin, you'll find yourself playing some rather short rallies. Topspin makes the ball drop into the court, so without it you'll be unable to hit decent shots and still keep them in. Every grip except the Continental supports topspin, more proof of how important topspin is. To add topspin to your ball, you will need to swing your racquet from low to high, not parallel to the parallel to the ground. This upward stroke will brush against the back of the ball to create topspin. It's important that you don't roll your wrist and try to add too much topspin, because you will then brush the top of the ball and hit it into the net. Just remember to bend your legs, use your other arm for balance, and aim 3-4 inches above the net. You'll use topspin much more often than you will hit flat shots because of the controlled power you can have, but you'll quickly get accustomed to the swing path.
Slice
Slices are a bit trickier to hit than, flats or topspin and should therefore not be attempted until you have a decent flat/topspin hit. The slice puts backspin on the ball, which makes the ball float in the air and bounce low. It makes for a great approach shot, as well as a defensive shot. The slice is like the opposite of the topspin so it is also very different, making it a shot that could throw your opponent off if they're not careful. Variety of shots, remember? To perform a backhand slice shot, start with a grip anywhere from an Eastern grip to a Continental grip. Lead with the same foot as your racquet arm, and lean on that leg, bending forwards and approching the ball. Swing the racquet back near your head and keep it upright. Bring the racquet down on the ball and follow through across your body. Make sure that the ball you're trying to hit isn't too low, because unlike a topspin shot, the swing path is from high to low. Make sure your wrist is locked the whole way through, and take note that to increase the power of a slice, don't swing faster or try to hit it harder, all you need to do is improve the timing. A forehand slice is similar, with the biggest difference being that you should hit it with an open stance. Keep you feet shoulder width apart, with your left foot farther in front for righties, and vice versa for lefties. Cut down on the ball from above, and finish across your body. Don't open the face too much, otherwise you'll get a floating ball high in air that others players just love to kill (a powerful shot usually close to the net, fun to hit).
Drop/Dump
The drop shot people should be pretty familiar with, it's a weak ball that just crosses the net and land very close to the net. The shot is much more difficult to hit than it looks, because of the perfect balance of power and control required to just get the ball and no more. The drop shot is very commonly combined with a slice to cause the ball to drop bounce even lower. A dump is when you are up at the net and just taps the ball so that it falls into the court. The drop shot can be used with any grip, but like volleying, the dump should be hit with a continental grip. When used effectively these shots can almost always guarantee a win, and even if they don't they can be used to wear your opponent down since they have to keep running up to return the shot.
Lob
The lob shot is the most underused shot in the game of tennis. Even some pros cannot perform good lobs, because they don't think that a lob shot is worth practicing for. In fact, it is a very deadly shot when it is performed well. Coming to the net and volleying is currently dominating the number of points won; part of the reason being that players cannot hit a decent lob shot, and rather risk hitting a much harder to hit passing shot. Think about it, a passing shot has quite a small aiming area since the player volleying usually covers 50-80% of the court. If the volleyer is good, he/she would have probably hit a good volley so that even if you manage to reach it, you would most likely be off balance. Small aiming area plus off balance equals a very hard to hit shot. A lob on the other hand, is a high shot that goes over the volleyers head and lands near the baseline. Indeed, it is tricky to be able to find the right angle and the power needed for the lob, but if you can land it in the baseline it will make it very difficult for the volleyer to reach it on time. The type of grip used for this shot doesn't matter, it is a good shot that can be used by all grips, but isn't.
Volley/Half Volley
Volleying is the main shot for winning points, and quickly too. It is when the player runs up to the net to hit the ball before it land on his side of the court. Although it is a relatively easy shot to perform, it is the timing of when to run up to the net that make the shot hard to do. A bad time to run up to the net is when you are rallying baseline to baseline. Another bad time is when you are not in control of the ball, meaning that you are on the defensive and just returning the ball, rather than on the offensive when you are aiming the ball. When you happen to be on the offensive, that is a good time to run up, as well as when your opponent hits a weak ball that lands short. Volleys are usually hit with a Continental grip, but slight variations are also used (a little more Eastern or Semi-Western). It is important to know that when you are hitting a volley, don't try too hard and don't bend your wrist. Always keep the racquet pointing up with your wrist locked. To hit lower shots, bend down instead of dropping your arm. The volley is more like blocking the ball, rather than hitting the ball, so don't try to muscle it past your opponent, just aim it away from him/her and you'll easily win. The backhand volley is usually a slice, while the forehand volley is often a flat hit. A half volley is usually a defensive shot, hitting the ball right after it bounces. Like a volley, you must stay low, bending your, knees, but it is much harder hit a successful half volley than a volley. Because it is so hard to hit, eye contact and proper technique are essential for a successful return. Aim hit the ball deep and relatively high, don't expect a winner just to return the ball is hard enough. As long as you can keep your opponent from hitting another offensive shot, you have achieved your goal.

3. Serving

Serving is the hardest aspect in tennis, and so it requires much more practice. Everyone's serve is slightly different, whether it's their toss, swing path, or follow through. Speed of serves also vary, as taller people will have a stronger serve than shorter people. Roddick is the current holder of the fastest serve, which was clocked at 156 miles per hour. The grip that is used for serving should be the Continental grip, but if it leans toward the Eastern grip a little bit, that's fine as well. I will explain how to serve in terms for a right-handed player, so for lefties just switch right to left. So you start behind the baseline, with your left foot pointing towards the right net pole and your right foot parallel to the baseline. A lot of times, the server bounces the ball a couple of times and leans forward. This is NOT the beginning of the serve, this is just to pump the server up. The serve begins when you lean back to toss the ball with the weight on the back foot (right foot). The toss should be practiced a lot, because later on you'll find that it is crucial to have your toss in a relative spot in the air. The toss for each serve is slightly different, but more on that later. After the toss, you should be taking your racquet back in a semi-circle swing, bending your knees, and preparing to hop into the ball. The more height you can get from a serve, the better, but jumping is not an effective method so stick with a hop. The racquet should be able to tap the back of your head when you bring it back. Don't bring it any lower until you are ready to make contact with the ball. When you are, drop the racquet head down, tuck your elbow in, shoot the racquet upwards and twist your wrist. Make contact with the ball at the highest point, and finish off with the racquet ending at the left side of your hip.

Flat
This most likely the first kind of serve you'll learn. It has no spin and cuts through the air very fast. It is this that gave it the nickname, "cannonball" serve. It makes for a great first serve because of the power, but not a good second serve due to its low accuracy. It needs to be aimed low, just over the net, in order for it to be in. A good time to use it is if you're trying to hit down the line, since the center of the net is lower. In terms of where the toss is for serves, imagine a clock in front of you, where 12 o'clock is directly up and 3 o'clock is to your right. A flat serve should have a toss at 12 o'clock, throwing more outwards when you are able to jump higher.
Slice
A slice serve is hit with sidespin, which requires the server to brush the back of the ball toward their dominant side with the racket. It is commonly hit with the Continental grip or the Eastern backhand grip. The ball is thrown slightly to the dominant side of the server then is struck laterally on the server's dominant side. For a right-handed player, a sliced serve's sidespin causes the ball to curve leftward. When the ball bounces, it skids and curves farther leftward. Since a slice serve has little or no topspin on it, it cannot be aimed high over the net and has little margin for error. Therefore, it is generally used as a first serve.
Kick
The kick (or topsin) serve is generated by tossing the ball over the head, then hitting it laterally on the server's non-dominant side brushing upward toward the dominant side. When hit correctly, the ball clears the net in a high arc with heavy topspin, causing the ball to dive into the service box. Upon hitting the surface of the court, the ball may bounce high directly toward the receiver for a kick serve. The physics of the spinning ball in flight involves the Magnus effect because the spinning ball creates a whirlpool of air around itself.

4. Types of Courts

Clay
A clay court is one of many different types of tennis court. Clay courts are made of crushed shale, stone or brick. The French Open uses clay courts, making it unique among the Grand Slam tournaments. Clay courts are more common in Continental Europe and Latin America than in the United States, Canada or Britain. Two types exist: red clay, the more common variety, and green clay, also known as "rubico", which is a harder surface. Although less expensive to construct than other types of tennis courts, the maintenance costs of clay are high as the surface must be rolled to preserve flatness. The water content must also be balanced; green courts are often sloped in order to allow water run-off.
Grass
A grass court is one of the four different types of court on which the sport of tennis, originally known as "lawn tennis", is played. Grass courts are made of grasses in different compositions depending on the tournament. Although grass courts are more traditional than other types of tennis courts, maintenance costs of grass courts are higher than those of hard courts and clay courts. Grass courts (in the absence of suitable covers) must be left for the day if rain appears, as the grass becomes very slippery when wet. Grass courts are most common in Britain, although the Northeastern United States also has some private grass courts.
Hard
Tennis hard courts are made of synthetic/acrylic layers on top of a concrete or asphalt foundation and can vary in color. These courts tend to play medium-fast to fast because there is little energy absorption by the court, like in grass courts. The ball tends to bounce high and players are able to apply many types of spin during play. Flat balls are favored on hard courts because of the extremely quick play style. Speed of rebound after tennis balls bounce on hard courts is determined by how much sand is in the synthetic/acrylic layer placed on top of the asphalt foundation. More sand will result in a slower bounce due to more friction. Of the Grand Slam tournaments, the US Open and Australian Open currently use hard courts and it is the predominant surface type used on the professional tour.
